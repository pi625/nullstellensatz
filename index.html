1. The Sumset Puzzle

Pick any 4 numbers from the set $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$. Add every possible pair together, wrapping around modulo 7. How many unique numbers do you get?

Let's define the notation simply. We have a prime $p$, and two sets $A$ and $B$ which are subsets of $\mathbb{Z}_p$. We define the sumset as: $$ A+B = \{a + b \pmod p \mid a \in A, b \in B\} $$

No matter how cleverly you pick $A$ and $B$, the size of $A+B$ never drops below $\min(p, |A|+|B|-1)$.

If you guessed that $|A+B|$ never drops below $\min(p, |A|+|B|-1)$, you just rediscovered the Cauchy-Davenport theorem, originally proved in 1935. Historically, proving this meant messy, case-by-case combinatorial analysis. How do you even think to prove this? I invite you to think about it for a second.

Proving this by brute force is a nightmare. Checking every pair takes $O(|A||B|)$ time, and the "wrapping around" of modulo arithmetic destroys our normal intuition about numbers. We need a structural guarantee.

But we aren't going to attack this head-on. Instead, we're going to take a step back and consider a seemingly completely unrelated question. As we build this theory more and more, we will eventually be able to sledgehammer this problem.

Think back to the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra. A non-zero univariate polynomial of degree $d$ has at most $d$ roots. It's a beautiful fact, but it feels... one-dimensional. What happens if we take this idea to $n$ dimensions?
